PCB Inspection and Repair Reference Notes

1. Solder Bridge
A solder bridge occurs when excess solder connects two pads or pins
that should not be electrically connected, usually due to too much
solder paste or poor reflow profile. It causes short circuits and
unpredictable board behavior.
Repair: Use desoldering braid or a solder sucker to remove the excess
solder, then re-inspect under magnification.
Inspection Method: Visual inspection under 10x-20x magnification, or
automated optical inspection (AOI).
Precautions: Ensure correct solder paste volume and reflow oven
temperature profile to prevent recurrence.

2. Cold Solder Joint
A cold solder joint happens when the solder does not fully melt or
bond to the pad and lead, often due to insufficient heat during
soldering, movement during cooling, or contamination. It appears
dull, grainy, or cracked instead of shiny and smooth.
Repair: Reheat the joint with a soldering iron and add a small amount
of fresh solder; keep the joint still while it cools.
Inspection Method: Visual inspection for dull/grainy texture; X-ray
inspection for hidden joints (e.g. BGA packages).
Precautions: Maintain proper iron temperature (~350°C for leaded
solder) and avoid disturbing the joint while cooling.

3. Missing Component
A missing component defect occurs when a required part (resistor,
capacitor, IC, etc.) was not placed during pick-and-place, often due
to a feeder jam or a programming error in the pick-and-place machine.
Repair: Manually place and solder the correct component per the BOM
(bill of materials) and schematic.
Inspection Method: Automated Optical Inspection (AOI) comparing
against the reference/golden board image; manual visual check.
Precautions: Regularly calibrate feeders and verify BOM/placement
program before production runs.

4. Component Misalignment
Misalignment occurs when a component is placed but rotated or shifted
from its intended footprint, often due to pick-and-place placement
error or component movement during reflow (tombstoning risk).
Repair: Reheat the solder joints and manually realign the component
using tweezers, then allow it to reflow/cool in the correct position.
Inspection Method: AOI with footprint overlay comparison; visual
inspection under magnification.
Precautions: Verify pick-and-place placement accuracy and stencil
alignment regularly.

5. Burn Mark / Thermal Damage
A burn mark indicates the board or a component was exposed to
excessive heat, often from a short circuit, incorrect component
rating, or a soldering iron held too long in one spot.
Repair: Replace the damaged component and inspect the surrounding
copper traces for damage; replace the board section if traces are
compromised.
Inspection Method: Visual inspection for discoloration; thermal
imaging during power-on testing.
Precautions: Verify component power ratings and check for short
circuits before applying power.

6. Short Circuit
A short circuit is an unintended low-resistance connection between
two nodes that should be isolated, often caused by a solder bridge,
conductive debris, or a design/layout error.
Recommended action: Do not power on the board until the short is
identified and cleared. Use a multimeter in continuity mode to trace
the short before resuming testing.
Inspection Method: Continuity testing, thermal imaging (a short often
heats up under power), visual inspection.
Precautions: Keep the work area free of conductive debris (solder
balls, wire clippings) and use ESD-safe handling.

7. Excess Solder
Excess solder appears as a large blob or dome around a joint. It can
hide underlying defects and increase the risk of bridging to
neighboring pads.
Repair: Remove excess solder with desoldering braid, leaving a
smooth, correctly-shaped fillet.
Inspection Method: Visual inspection; AOI solder-volume checks.
Precautions: Calibrate solder paste stencil thickness and dispenser
volume settings.

General IPC-A-610 Note
IPC-A-610 is the industry-accepted standard for acceptability of
electronic assemblies, defining Class 1 (general), Class 2
(dedicated service), and Class 3 (high-reliability) criteria for
solder joints, component placement, and overall workmanship. When in
doubt about whether a defect is acceptable, refer to the applicable
IPC-A-610 class for the product being inspected.
